The problem

Defects were reaching a platform that couldn't afford them

The Smart Plan Suite is a 5G-ready charging platform: microservices-based, integration-heavy, and customer-facing. Defect handling was reactive at the time. Issues were logged and passed along, but no one owned them from discovery to verified closure, and coverage had gaps in the places that mattered most, the integration points and data flows between services. The same kinds of defect kept recurring across releases.

The solution

Own the defect lifecycle, and test where the risk lives

I took ownership of the full defect lifecycle in JIRA, from triage and root-cause analysis through escalation to closure, so defects stopped falling through the gaps between development and services. Alongside that, I reworked the SIT test strategy around risk rather than volume, concentrating test design on the integration points, API interactions, and edge behaviours where defects were actually starting, and I documented the integration flows and API interactions (REST, Diameter) so the whole team had one reference to work from.

The result
Post-deployment defects came down by roughly 40%, and meaningful test coverage rose 30%. That came from testing the things that actually carried risk, not from writing more tests. Owning the lifecycle end to end made defect handling predictable instead of reactive.

The problem

Knowledge lived in people's heads, not in the system

On a complex platform with distributed teams, ramp-up depended on informal knowledge transfer, with senior engineers pausing their own work to walk newcomers through undocumented processes. It was slow, inconsistent, and costly every time someone new joined.

The solution

Build the knowledge infrastructure once, maintain it for good

I built traceability matrices, structured Confluence knowledge systems, and onboarding material designed for reuse, so good practice didn't depend on any one person being available. Everything was built to be updated as the platform evolved, rather than rebuilt from scratch.

The result
New-team ramp-up dropped by approximately 25%, measured through team-lead feedback and faster time-to-first-contribution, and I became the primary technical reference for how the platform behaved.
